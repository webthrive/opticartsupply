Studio Easel vs. Tabletop Easel: Which One Is Right for You?

By Colin · 21 May 2025 · 7 min read

Trying to choose between a studio easel and a tabletop easel? Learn the key differences, pros, and best use cases to find the right fit for your workspace and art style.

When you’re ready to begin painting, you might wonder which type of easel suits you best. Do you go for a tall, sturdy studio easel that stands on the floor, or do you pick a tabletop easel that can fit on your desk? Both options have some fantastic benefits, and both come with a few drawbacks, too. In this blog, we’ll walk through the key differences, share tips to help you pick what’s right for your creative style, and even talk about the role of linen canvases in your artwork. By the time you’re done reading, you’ll feel confident about which easel to choose for your painting needs.

The Basics of a Studio Easel

A studio easel is the kind of easel you’ve probably seen in professional art studios or classrooms. It’s the big wooden or metal stand that sits on the floor and supports larger canvases. Many artists love studio easels because they’re great for big projects and offer a sturdy platform. They typically feature adjustable angles, so you can tilt your canvas to a comfortable position, and they often hold your painting materials at the perfect height.

If you often paint on large canvases, a studio easel can give you the freedom to step back and see your entire composition at once. This is a huge plus if you enjoy making big, bold strokes and you don’t want to feel cramped at a small desk. In a way, a studio easel is like having the right equipment for a specialized procedure—if you know you’re going to be working on large-scale art, you want a tool that supports that scope.

Key perks of a studio easel include:

  • Stability for large canvases
  • Room for different painting angles
  • A professional feel that can spark creativity
  • Freedom to step back and view your work from a distance

Potential downsides:

  • Takes up more floor space
  • Usually heavier and harder to move around
  • Might be costly, especially for high-quality models

The Basics of a Tabletop Easel

A tabletop easel is smaller and designed to sit on a table or a desk. It’s often foldable and lightweight, making it easy to store and carry. If you have limited space, a tabletop easel can be a real lifesaver because you don’t need to dedicate a chunk of your room to art equipment. Once you’re done painting, you can just tuck it away in a closet or under the bed.

Tabletop easels might not hold massive canvases, but they’re great for small to medium projects. If you like painting mini-portraits or smaller landscapes, or you simply prefer working at a desk, this option is a good fit. Think of a tabletop easel as a compact companion—much like a handy tool you keep in a small drawer but that offers plenty of function when you bring it out.

Key perks of a tabletop easel include:

  • Compact and easy to store
  • Lightweight and portable
  • Great for smaller projects
  • Often more affordable than studio easels

Potential downsides:

  • Not suitable for very large canvases
  • Limited adjustability compared to bigger easels
  • Less stable, especially if the base is small

Who Should Go for a Studio Easel?

Consider a studio easel if you’re the kind of person who loves painting large. Maybe you’ve got a dedicated art room, a garage workshop, or even a spacious corner in your living space where a bigger easel can stand tall. A studio easel gives you a platform that feels professional, and you can adjust it for comfortable painting angles. This type of easel also works well for heavier canvases. If you’re someone who wants to do layered oil paintings or bold acrylic pieces on a big surface, you’ll likely appreciate the stability a studio easel offers.

A studio easel can also be helpful if you’re focusing on advanced techniques that demand a solid support. When you’re experimenting with thick layers of paint or you need precision in your brushstrokes, you want something that won’t wobble every time you apply pressure. Having a sturdy piece of equipment can really help you concentrate on your craft. It’s kind of like how you want to avoid too much movement when you’re performing any delicate procedure—you need stability for the best results.

Who Should Go for a Tabletop Easel?

On the other hand, if space is tight or you prefer a more portable solution, a tabletop easel might be your best friend. It’s especially good for casual painters or hobbyists who don’t plan on working with giant canvases. For example, if you share a small apartment with roommates or if you travel frequently, a tabletop easel is easy to set up and break down.

You might also enjoy the flexibility of painting at your kitchen table or on a desk in your bedroom. After you finish creating a small masterpiece, just fold up the easel and store it away. Tabletop easels are a great choice if your painting style involves smaller details, like detailed watercolor pieces or miniature landscapes. They often cost less than studio easels, so they’re also a good option if you’re on a budget.

How to Decide Which Easel Fits Your Needs?

Picking the right easel can feel like choosing between two amazing tools. Here are a few questions to help you decide:

How much space do you have?

  • If you have an entire room or a roomy corner, a studio easel might fit your setup well.
  • If you only have a small desk or table, consider a tabletop easel.

What size canvases do you usually paint on?

  • If you love painting huge landscapes or portraits, you’ll want the stability of a studio easel.
  • If you mostly create smaller pieces, a tabletop easel should suffice.

Do you need to move your easel around?

  • If you attend art workshops, or if you like painting outside, a lightweight tabletop easel is easy to carry.
  • If you’re mostly staying in one spot, go for a studio easel.

What’s your budget?

  • Studio easels can be pricier, but they’re often built to hold heavier canvases securely.
  • Tabletop easels are generally cheaper and still get the job done for small to medium art.

Are you planning to use linen canvases or heavier surfaces?

  • Linen canvases can add weight, so a studio easel can offer more support for larger linen canvases.
  • Smaller linen canvases should do fine on a sturdy tabletop easel.

Consider these questions carefully to pick the easel that aligns with your goals. Practical Tips for Using Each Easel

For a Studio Easel

  • Adjust the angle to reduce strain on your neck and back. Painting comfortably can improve your creativity because you won’t be distracted by aches.
  • Secure your canvas properly. A large canvas might shift if not clamped well.
  • Keep your workspace organized by storing brushes and paints in reachable spots. You don’t want to step over tubes of paint whenever you move around.

For a Tabletop Easel

  • Use a sturdy table that won’t wobble. Even though a tabletop easel is lightweight, you still want to minimize shaking when you’re working on details.
  • Protect your table’s surface with a cloth or plastic sheet. Paint drips can happen, and it’s much easier to clean a cover than to scrub paint off your furniture.
  • Pay attention to lighting. Tabletop easels might be used in smaller rooms, so a good lamp or natural window light can help you see your colors accurately.

Why This Decision Matters?

You might wonder why it’s worth taking time to pick the right easel. The short answer is that your art space should support your flow, not hamper it. When you’re in the middle of a creative moment, the last thing you want is to fuss with a wobbling stand or a canvas that’s too big for your easel. A solid setup can help you focus purely on your art.

Investing in the right easel is similar to investing in the right tools in any professional field: it saves you frustration and allows you to direct your energy toward what truly matters—making art. Whether you’re painting for fun or building a creative career, having equipment that fits your style can inspire you to paint more often and with greater confidence.

Final Thoughts

If you dream of large-scale paintings and have enough room, a studio easel might be your best partner. If you’re short on space or you love creating compact pieces, a tabletop easel could be your most convenient option.

At the end of the day, the goal is to nurture your creativity. Pick the tools that make you excited to paint, and don’t be afraid to try new things. Whether you choose a grand studio easel or a humble tabletop easel, what matters most is that you’re ready to express yourself on that canvas—big, small, or somewhere in between.

Happy painting, and may your next masterpiece bring you as much joy creating it as you hope to bring to those who see it!
